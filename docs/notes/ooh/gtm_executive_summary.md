# Go-to-market executive summary, in five layers

**aimez** | strategy summary | June 2026

Each layer stands alone in conversation. Start at whichever layer the listener
cares about; the layers below are the support.

## Layer 1: Product

A measured decision surface for pedestrian movement in Manhattan. A street
graph where every block carries validated layers (street pressure, tree
canopy, surface heat) and an engine that returns five comparable routes with
per-block statistics and confidence in one call. Consumers see routes; the
asset is the surface underneath, which knows where people walk and how each
block feels.

Proof: the Grand Central to Carnegie Hall case (`fig_case_gc_carnegie`), one
walk five ways with the cost of each choice quantified per block.

## Layer 2: Moat

The validation and provenance discipline, not the routing code. Every layer
carries a manifest with input hashes; every claim has an allowed and
not-allowed list; audits are cheap enough to run the same morning a doubt is
raised. Live proof from this week: a tree-level audit caught the canopy layer
crediting side-street trees to avenue corners, withdrew a published claim and
replaced the mechanism within one day (`fig_case_upwind`). A competitor can
re-weight public data; they cannot shortcut the discipline that catches that
class of error.

## Layer 3: Beachhead, out-of-home advertising

OOH sells foot-traffic exposure as its entire currency and buys data products
rather than apps. The offer: a comfort-weighted exposure index per panel
location with a confidence score and a provenance trail. Incumbent counts from
mobile-location panels say how many; the surface explains why a block draws or
repels walkers and how long they linger. Pilot design: one corridor, one
metric, one media owner, success defined as the index reordering inventory in a
way the current data missed.

Proof of the underlying mechanics: the Village dog-park case
(`fig_case_village`), where tree cover measurably reorders route choice and the
flip threshold is quantified (about 11 DBH-inches per 100 m at the working
shade assumption).

## Layer 4: Adjacent markets

Behind the beachhead, in priority order: outdoor-workforce heat compliance
under tightening occupational rules, municipal resilience and transportation
offices that currently buy static heat reports, real estate walkability and
comfort indices, last-mile logistics routing for couriers and temperature
sensitive cargo. Each reuses the same surface with a different cost weighting;
none requires a consumer user base.

## Layer 5: Motion and asks

Sales motion: paid pilot study first, then a per-market annual data license
keyed to inventory count. Land one media owner, expand to brand-side buyers who
want independent exposure quality before committing spend. Research leg: NSF
SBIR Phase I pitch submitted on block-scale pedestrian heat exposure with
calibrated uncertainty. Asks, in order: go-to-market judgment from an operator
who has done it, adjacent paid work near decision systems while the venture
matures, a milestone-gated investor look after the pitch lands and a pilot
signs.

## Honest caveats

OOH measurement is standards-driven, so an independent index has to be
benchmarked before owners adopt it. Comfort-to-dwell is a hypothesis the first
pilot exists to test. Mobile-location panels out-scale us, so the pitch stays
on explanatory quality rather than raw coverage. All heat readings are surface
temperature until street-level measurement exists.
