Public · Executive summary

aimez: measured fields on pedestrian graphs

This page orients investors and partners to the Midtown Manhattan demonstration: how public camera feeds and open city data become a routable graph, what the canonical figures show, and where to try the live system.

Problem and product shape

Standard map routing optimizes distance or time. Urban walking also depends on local pressure: crowding, sidewalk capacity, construction, and station egress. aimez builds a measured stress field on a pedestrian graph and routes through it, so “better path” means lower measured exposure at comparable hop count—not a black-box comfort score.

The first applied surface is a fixed Midtown corridor (40th–59th Streets, Lexington to 8th Avenue): ~300 nodes, public NYC Open Data for supply-side capacity, and a camera-derived demand field. The same architecture extends to other corridors once sensing and graph data are in place.

What is validated vs open

  • Validated in-repo: Substrate-level route dominance on measured stress; orthogonality of capacity vs camera stress (r ≈ 0); internal lived-knowledge polarity check on 12 blocks; structured figure reproducibility from locked decision records.
  • Open / partnership: Independent pedestrian volume at block resolution (e.g. commercial mobility vendors); behavioral A/B on real walkers; real-time feeds beyond historical MTA ridership patterns.
