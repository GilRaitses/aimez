This deck walks the published figure set in the order the substrate is actually built: measure the field, show its topology, add supply, then turn on routing.

Each section moves from what we are doing, to the figure that makes it visible, to what that result unlocks next.

Next: the problem the corridor graph is meant to make testable.

§0 · Problem

Urban walking is a graph with hidden load

We treat Midtown as a fixed pedestrian graph so route behavior can be compared on measured quantities instead of narrative claims about how the city “feels.”

  • The graph stays fixed while edge weights change, which separates topology from the field living on it.
  • Local pressure—crowding, capacity, construction, egress—shows up on edges before any recommendation is made.
  • Claims stay at corridor scale; we do not assert a general law for all of Manhattan.

Next: how three independent measurement streams are fused into one substrate.

§0 · Methods

Three measurement streams become one substrate

The substrate is a cross-domain synthesis: public sensing for demand, open civic data for supply, and field calibration so the combined field can be audited.

  • Camera-derived stress scores anchor the demand side to observable street conditions at block scale.
  • NYC Open Data capacity layers encode sidewalk width, sheds, frontage load, and related supply constraints on cross-street edges.
  • Lived-knowledge and reference-block checks provide an external polarity test where block-level pedestrian counts are not publicly available.

§2 · Emergent topology

Stress field structure on the graph

Before path algorithms run, the corridor already shows organized pressure: where it is hot, where it is cool, and where hot spots sit inside larger regions.

  • Each Voronoi cell inherits one camera’s score, so block-scale demand is visible as a tessellated surface.
  • Gradients and localized peaks are read as topology of measured pressure, not as route suggestions.
  • Colormap and value range are locked in a decision record so figures remain comparable across renders.

Next: the supply substrate from civic open data—and whether it duplicates demand.

§2 · Supply substrate

Cross-street capacity from civic open data

Supply describes how much physical corridor capacity an edge carries, decomposed into layers that can be turned on or off for policy scenarios.

  • Sidewalk width, active sheds, and frontage load each enter as a multiplicative factor on cross-street edges.
  • The combined layer is the effective denominator the routing cost can read against measured stress.
  • Additional layers (stations, closures, open streets) extend the same compositional pattern in the full model.

Next: the orthogonality check—whether supply and demand measure the same thing twice.

Outcome · Validation preview

Supply and demand measure different things

At cross-street edges, camera stress and modeled capacity are nearly uncorrelated, so the routing layer is not relabeling one signal under two names.

  • Pearson r ≈ −0.014 on 228 cross-street edges in the published orthogonality figure (§4.1).
  • Orthogonality is necessary but not sufficient: it rules out redundancy, not automatic ground truth for either layer alone.

Next: §1—navigation built on top of this substrate, starting with a single origin–destination comparison.

§1 · Navigation layer

Routing reads the substrate—not the reverse

Shortest-hop routing uses the graph alone; stress-aware routing reads the measured field on edges while holding the same topology.

  • We report accumulated stress and redistribution on the graph, not inferred pedestrian preference or stated comfort.
  • A dominance claim means the stress-aware path scores better on locked substrate metrics at the same hop count for the demonstration pair.
  • Behavioral validation with independent foot-traffic or panel data remains an open partnership surface.

Next: §3—operator summary (stress–flow coupling and centrality reorganization).

§3 · Operator

How the graph operator reorganizes under stress

The same perturbation that changes two routes also changes which nodes and edges the corridor treats as influential under alternative centrality and flow readouts.

  • Laplacian eigenvectors describe slow modes of the corridor; the Fiedler vector aligns with north–south flow in the published spectral figure.
  • Stress-weighting can reshuffle top-centrality sets almost completely (e.g. Jaccard 0.00 between top-20 eigenvector sets in the locked comparison).
  • Operator panels support research partners; they are not required to accept the routing demonstration on their own.

§4 · Validation

What is checked publicly—and what is still open

Validation separates internal consistency checks (published with limits) from external pedestrian-volume ground truth (partnership-dependent).

  • Orthogonality confirms supply and demand are not redundant at the cross-street edge level.
  • The lived-knowledge audit tests polarity predictions on twelve reference blocks where the author has strong prior expectations.
  • Independent block-level pedestrian counts and behavioral trials are explicitly not claimed in this deck.
