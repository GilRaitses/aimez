pax — research platform post 
aimez.ai is a one-person AI research lab. The work is about grounding AI systems in measured physical environments and documenting exactly where that grounding holds and where it doesn't.

pax routes pedestrians on the full Manhattan island walk graph (4,567 nodes, 15,788 edges) using five cost encodings: shortest, crowd-avoiding, shade-seeking, comfort-aware, and adaptive. Physical world model layers — crowd pressure from NYC DOT cameras, surface heat from Landsat, mean radiant temperature from a precomputed SOLWEIG physics model, street-tree canopy from LiDAR — drive Dijkstra edge weights.

The whitepaper documents a specific gap and a partial closure: comfort-aware and adaptive routes now use a 31-bin time-of-day thermal model as live edge weights. The gap between that precomputed model and live atmospheric data is not closed. The paper traces it to source, quantifies it, and proposes three closure mechanisms. This is one city, one season, five encodings on an open-data testbed.

Every narrated route number is labeled as live, partially live, or deferred.

ORCAST — research platform post 
New preprint from aimez.ai: evidence-bounded encounter forecasting for cetacean observation data.

ORCAST forecasts killer whale encounter probability for the San Juan Islands from acoustic and sighting records. The system uses gate-bounded confidence display: every probability shown on the map is traceable to a source measurement. Human-in-the-loop moderation gates any forecast that falls outside the evidence envelope.

The second ORCAST whitepaper documents a general grounding architecture for physical-world AI systems: measuring the rate at which model outputs bind to source citations vs. generate unsupported claims.

Live platform: https://orcast-h0.vercel.app
